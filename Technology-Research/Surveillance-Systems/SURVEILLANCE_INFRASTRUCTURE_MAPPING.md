## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**The US has developed a vast ALPR data-sharing infrastructure that enables mass surveillance while circumventing local privacy laws.**

### Key Findings

1. **Interstate ALPR Networks**
   - **LEARN** (Law Enforcement Automatic Recognition Network) — Motorola Vigilant platform
   - **Flock network** — Decentralized camera-owner sharing (100,000+ cameras)
   - **Fusus RTCC** — Axon-owned command center (connecting multiple sources)
   - **State/local systems** — California, New York, Texas, Florida, Illinois, Washington, etc.

2. **Federal Agency Access**
   - **FBI** — Direct integration with state/local ALPR systems (warrants sometimes required)
   - **ICE** — Immigration enforcement ALPR access (sanctuary law workarounds)
   - **Border Patrol** — "Back-door" access via state systems (unauthorized sharing)
   - **DEA** — Narcotics enforcement ALPR queries
   - **Secret Service** — Presidential protection ALPR network

3. **Data Retention Variation (Chaos)**
   - **Best practice:** 30 days (CA, NY, some others)
   - **Standard:** 90–180 days (most states)
   - **Worst:** 365 days (San Jose PD) → 730 days (some federal systems)
   - **Unregulated:** Private camera networks (Flock) — no legal retention limit

4. **Privacy Law Violations (Documented)**
   - **California SB 34 (2016):** Bans out-of-state/federal sharing; violated by Flock/ICE
   - **Washington:** Border Patrol unauthorized "back-door" access (2025)
   - **Illinois:** Federal agencies bypassing legal restrictions
   - **Massachusetts:** ALPR searches without warrant (upheld, narrowly)

5. **Vulnerable Populations**
   - **Immigrants:** ICE tracking via ALPR (sanctuary law violations)
   - **Abortion seekers:** Out-of-state ALPR tracking (anti-abortion states)
   - **Protestors:** Protest movement vehicle tracking (surveillance)
   - **Journalists:** Media vehicle tracking (chilling effect)

## SECTION 1: NATIONAL ALPR NETWORKS

### LEARN (Law Enforcement Automatic Recognition Network)

**Operator:** Motorola Solutions (Vigilant ALPR platform)

**Description:**
- Largest US ALPR network (law enforcement)
- Centralized database of license plate hits
- Integration with state/local police (direct access)
- Federal agency integration (FBI, ICE, DEA)

**Coverage:**
- All 50 states (estimated)
- Millions of plates scanned daily
- Historical data: Varies by jurisdiction (30–365 days)

**Federal Access:**
- FBI: Direct integration (some warrant requirement)
- ICE: Direct integration (minimal oversight)
- DEA: Query access (narcotics enforcement)
- Secret Service: Presidential protection queries
- Other federal agencies: Via interagency agreements

**Privacy Concerns:**
- Centralized data = mass surveillance potential
- Federal integration = bypasses state/local privacy controls
- Minimal transparency (police logs not public)
- Chilling effect (vehicles can be tracked retroactively)

**Epstein Investigation Angle:**
- If Epstein network vehicles were in LEARN system (2015–2019), historical searches could reconstruct movements
- Federal access = did investigators search LEARN for Epstein vehicles?
- **Missing evidence:** LEARN query logs (Epstein case, 2019–2025)

### Flock Safety Network

**Operator:** Flock Safety (decentralized + cloud)

**Description:**
- Decentralized ALPR network (100,000+ cameras)
- Camera owners + law enforcement participation
- Cloud-based search (cameras upload plate data)
- Designed for municipal control + privacy

**Coverage:**
- 40+ states (estimated), 1,000+ jurisdictions
- Millions of cameras (residential, commercial, municipal)
- Growth trajectory: ~20% annually

**Federal Access (By Design Restriction):**
- Flock historically BLOCKED federal agency integration
- But: "Back-door" access documented (ICE, Border Patrol, 2025)
- California SB 34 compliance: Flock blocks out-of-state sharing (mostly)

**Privacy Model:**
- Decentralized (data on camera owners' systems)
- Search transparency (queries logged, reviewed)
- Municipal control (cities can restrict searches)
- Limited federal access (by policy, not law)

**Recent Violations (Documented):**

**Ventura County, CA (Feb 2026):**
- Flock revealed that federal agencies BYPASSED data-sharing restrictions
- "Flock has blocked federal agencies from engaging in any data sharing relationships with California law enforcement agencies"
- BUT: Federal access still occurring (investigation ongoing)

**Washington State (Nov 2025):**
- University of Washington report: Border Patrol had "back-door" access to local ALPR data
- Unauthorized sharing (May–Aug 2025)
- 10+ jurisdictions affected

**Epstein Investigation Angle:**
- Flock's privacy model makes it less useful for federal surveillance
- If Epstein case involved Flock-only ALPR data, federal investigators had limited access
- **Question:** Did Axon ALPR (more federal-friendly) replace Flock for this reason?

### Fusus (Axon-Owned RTCC Platform)

**Operator:** Axon Enterprise (acquired Feb 2024)

**Description:**
- Real-Time Crime Center (RTCC) command + control
- Integrates multiple data sources (ALPR, body cams, 911, dispatch)
- AI-driven analytics
- Multi-agency information sharing

**Coverage:**
- 200+ law enforcement agencies (estimated)
- 50+ states
- Growing rapidly post-Axon integration

**Federal Integration:**
- FBI partnership (expected post-acquisition)
- ICE integration pathway (likely)
- DEA access (probable)
- Data sharing with 911/dispatch (Carbyne)

**Privacy Concerns:**
- Centralized architecture (federal-friendly)
- Minimal transparency (proprietary system)
- AI automation (bias risk, audit prevention)
- Vendor lock-in (Axon controls access)

**Epstein Investigation Angle:**
- Fusus integration could have enabled federal surveillance of Epstein network
- Axon's reputation risk if Epstein case exposed via Fusus data
- **Question:** Is Fusus being used to suppress evidence access?

## SECTION 2: STATE-BY-STATE DATA RETENTION POLICIES

### Retention Landscape (Chaos)

**Ultra-Privacy (30 Days):**
- California (SB 34, 2016)
- New York (state law)
- Some municipalities (local ordinances)

**Privacy-Focused (60–90 Days):**
- Illinois (restricted use)
- Virginia (limited criminal matters)
- Massachusetts
- Others (various)

**Standard (90–180 Days):**
- Most states (no specific law)
- Federal default (implied)
- Motorola Vigilant default

**Surveillance-Friendly (365+ Days):**
- San Jose PD (365 days — extreme outlier)
- Federal systems (730 days+)
- Some LEARN nodes (state-dependent)

**No Legal Limit:**
- Private camera networks (Flock customers, unregulated)
- Unmandated systems (municipal choice)

## SECTION 4: 4TH AMENDMENT IMPLICATIONS

### Carpenter v. United States (2018) — Controlling Precedent

**Holding:** Accessing historical cell-site location information (CSLI) is a search requiring a warrant.

**Relevance to ALPR:**
- Historical ALPR searches = analogous to CSLI
- Movement pattern = reveals private life
- Reasonable expectation of privacy in vehicle location
- **Question:** Does Carpenter require warrant for ALPR historical searches?

### Current State of Law (Uncertain)

**Massachusetts (2020):**
- Court held warrantless ALPR use constitutional
- BUT: Limited to specific facts (time, scope, location)
- Narrow holding (not blanket approval)

**Norfolk, VA (Feb 2026):**
- Court dismissed 4th Amendment ALPR challenge
- Affirmed city's ALPR system as constitutional
- **But:** Narrow grounds; full precedent TBD

**Uncertainty:**
- No Supreme Court guidance on ALPR (post-Carpenter)
- Lower courts split on applicability
- Federal agencies acting as if Carpenter doesn't apply

### Defense Vulnerability (If Epstein Case Goes to Trial)

**Prosecution Risk:**
- If ALPR evidence obtained without warrant, exclusion possible
- Defenses could challenge ALPR searches (motion to suppress)
- Carpenter argument: Historical ALPR = search requiring warrant
- **Mitigation:** Prosecute with other evidence; don't rely solely on ALPR
